Overview & Principles

Operative obstetric analgesia/anesthesia encompasses methods to relieve pain during labor, operative vaginal delivery, and cesarean section. Goal: maternal comfort, safety, minimal fetal depression, and maintenance of labor progress.

📌 General Principles
Pain is subjective; analgesia should be tailored. Most medications cross placenta → monitor fetal heart rate. Avoid aspirin (bleeding risk).
🎯 Types
Systemic (opioids, sedatives), Regional (epidural, spinal, combined), Local (pudendal, perineal infiltration), General anesthesia.
Key concept: Epidural analgesia is the most effective method for labor pain, but requires IV access, fluid bolus, and continuous monitoring.

Systemic Analgesia (Opioids)

  • Common opioids: Meperidine (Demerol), Fentanyl, Morphine, Nalbuphine (Nubain), Butorphanol (Stadol).
  • Route: IV (preferred for rapid onset) or IM. Onset: IM 30-45 min; IV 5-10 min.
  • Fetal effects: Respiratory depression, decreased variability, reduced beat-to-beat variability. Give at least 1-3 hours before delivery to minimize neonatal depression.
  • Nursing interventions: Monitor respiratory rate, sedation level, FHR. Keep naloxone (Narcan) available for neonatal resuscitation.
  • Narcotic antagonists: Naloxone (0.01 mg/kg IM/IV/ET) reverses respiratory depression in newborn.
⚠️ Caution: IV opioids can cause maternal hypotension, nausea, vomiting, and pruritus. PCA allows self‑administration with safety limits.

Regional Anesthesia (Epidural & Spinal)

🔹 Epidural Anesthesia
Most common for labor. Catheter placed in epidural space (L3-L4). Provides continuous pain relief. Local anesthetic (bupivacaine, ropivacaine) + opioid (fentanyl). Onset 10-20 min. Advantages: titratable, patient‑controlled (PCEA).
🔸 Spinal Anesthesia
Single injection into subarachnoid space (L4-L5). Rapid onset (2-5 min), dense motor block. Used for cesarean section or operative vaginal delivery. Avoid for prolonged labor due to short duration.
🔹 Combined Spinal‑Epidural (CSE)
Fast onset of spinal with ability to extend via epidural catheter. Ideal for early labor then prolonged.
📋 Nursing care: Pre‑procedure: IV fluid bolus (500-1000 mL LR), baseline vitals, fetal monitoring, informed consent. Post‑procedure: monitor BP every 5-15 min, assess sensory/motor level, bladder distension, pruritus, nausea.
🚨 Complications: Hypotension (most common), post‑dural puncture headache (spinal), urinary retention, pruritus (opioids), fever, rarely epidural hematoma or abscess.

Local Infiltration & Pudendal Block

  • Local infiltration: Lidocaine (1%) injected into perineal area for episiotomy or laceration repair. Onset immediate, short duration.
  • Pudendal nerve block: Injected at ischial spine to anesthetize perineum, vulva, lower vagina (spares uterus). Indicated for second stage, low forceps, vacuum, or episiotomy. Onset 5-10 min, duration ~1 hour.
  • Nursing considerations: Monitor for local anesthetic toxicity (drowsiness, metallic taste, seizures). Avoid intravascular injection.
  • Advantages: No maternal hypotension, no motor block of legs, minimal fetal effect.
Clinical pearl: Pudendal block does not relieve pain from uterine contractions – only for delivery and repair.

General Anesthesia & Indications

  • Indications: Emergency cesarean (fetal distress, uterine rupture, massive hemorrhage), contraindication to regional (coagulopathy, sepsis, patient refusal), failed regional, maternal cardiac disease, need for rapid delivery.
  • Technique: Rapid sequence induction (cricoid pressure) to prevent aspiration. Agents: thiopental, propofol, ketamine. Succinylcholine for paralysis. Maintenance with volatile anesthetics (sevoflurane, isoflurane).
  • Risks: Maternal: aspiration (Mendelson’s syndrome), failed intubation, uterine atony, awareness, malignant hyperthermia. Fetal: neonatal depression (crosses placenta), reduced Apgar scores.
  • Nursing role: Pre‑oxygenation, left uterine displacement, aspiration precautions, assist with intubation, monitor neonatal resuscitation readiness.
⚠️ Mendelson’s syndrome: Aspiration of gastric contents → chemical pneumonitis. Prevention: non‑particulate antacids, H2 blockers, rapid sequence induction.

Complications & Nursing Care

🩸 Hypotension (epidural/spinal)
Mechanism: sympathetic blockade → vasodilation. Prevention: IV fluid bolus, left lateral tilt. Treatment: reposition, fluids, ephedrine (5-10 mg IV) or phenylephrine.
🤕 Post‑dural puncture headache (PDPH)
Caused by CSF leak after spinal or accidental dural puncture. Worse upright, relieved supine. Treatment: hydration, caffeine, blood patch (epidural).
🩺 Urinary retention
Frequent with epidural due to sacral nerve block. Need indwelling catheter or intermittent straight cath.
🌡️ Fever (epidural)
Non‑infectious hyperthermia; rule out chorioamnionitis.
🧴 Local anesthetic toxicity
Signs: perioral numbness, metallic taste, tinnitus, seizures, cardiac arrest. Treat: lipid emulsion (Intralipid).
🧠 High/total spinal
Spread of spinal anesthetic to upper thoracic/cervical → apnea, hypotension, bradycardia. Emergency: airway management, vasopressors, CPR.
🚨 Nursing priority: Monitor BP and respiratory status every 5-15 minutes after neuraxial block; treat hypotension immediately to preserve placental perfusion.

Mnemonics & Summary

📌 “E.S.P.” – Regional types
Epidural, Spinal, Pudendal.
💊 Opioid reversal
Naloxone (Narcan) – dose 0.01 mg/kg in newborn.
🩸 Hypotension management mnemonic “F.O.R.M.”
Fluids, Oxygen, Reposition (left tilt), Medications (ephedrine/phenylephrine).
⚠️ Contraindications to regional
Patient refusal, coagulopathy, infection at site, hypovolemia, increased ICP, sepsis.
📊 CSE → Combined Spinal‑Epidural
Fast onset + continuous extension.
🧪 Local anesthetic toxicity “L.A.T.E.”
Lidocaine-type drugs, Altered mental status, Tinnitus, ECG changes → lipid emulsion.
